Schools in Assam: A Complete Guide to the Education System in the State

By Sahajul  ·  Tezpur, Assam  ·  January 2025  ·  10 min read

I have lived in Assam for most of my life, and I have been writing about education here for fourteen years. That combination gives me, I think, a perspective on Assam's school system that is grounded in both the data and the daily reality that the data cannot always capture. Assam is a state of enormous contrasts — vibrant cities and remote forest villages, some of the most culturally rich communities in India alongside some of the country's most severe development challenges.

Overview of Assam's Education System

Assam has a large and complex school education system. The state has tens of thousands of schools registered in the UDISE database, ranging from government primary schools in remote char (river island) villages to large private schools in Guwahati and Dibrugarh. The overall literacy rate in Assam is around 72 percent — below the national average but with significant internal variation, with urban districts near Guwahati showing rates considerably higher than remote tribal or tea garden districts.

The State Board and Curriculum

Most government schools in Assam are affiliated to the Board of Secondary Education, Assam (SEBA) for Classes 9 and 10, and the Assam Higher Secondary Education Council (AHSEC) for Classes 11 and 12. Private schools may be affiliated to CBSE or ICSE instead. The medium of instruction in government schools is typically Assamese, Bengali, or Bodo depending on the district, with some schools offering English medium education.

Government Schools in Assam

Government schools in Assam employ a large number of teachers under various categories — regularised government employees, LP (Lower Primary) and UP (Upper Primary) school teachers, and teachers employed under various centrally sponsored schemes. Teacher availability and attendance have been persistent challenges, particularly in remote blocks. The state has made efforts to address this through mobile monitoring systems and administrative pressure, with mixed results.

Tea Garden Schools

One distinctive feature of Assam's school landscape is the presence of tea garden schools — schools located within or near tea estates and catering to the children of tea garden workers. These schools have a complex history: many were originally established by tea companies and have been gradually brought into the state school system. The quality of education in tea garden communities has historically been poor, with low literacy rates and high dropout rates — a reflection of the broader socioeconomic marginalisation of tea garden communities.

Northeast India Context

Assam's position within Northeast India gives it a distinctive educational context. The state has significant diversity in language, religion, and ethnicity, with communities ranging from the Bodo people (a Scheduled Tribe with their own language and cultural traditions) to Bengali-speaking communities in Barak Valley to various hill tribes. This diversity means that a one-size-fits-all approach to education policy has never worked well for Assam, and the best schools in the state are those that have found ways to be responsive to their specific communities.
