The Right to Education Act Explained: What It Means for Your Child

By Sahajul  ·  Tezpur, Assam  ·  January 2025  ·  10 min read

The Right to Education Act, passed by the Indian Parliament in 2009 and coming into effect in 2010, was a landmark piece of legislation. It made free and compulsory education a fundamental right for every child between the ages of six and fourteen. After years of education being framed as a Directive Principle — something the state should strive toward but was not constitutionally obligated to deliver — the Act made it enforceable. A parent whose child was denied admission to a school or expelled without due process now had a legal remedy.

Core Provisions

The Act's core provisions established several important rights and requirements. Every child between 6 and 14 has the right to be admitted to a neighbourhood school. No child can be held back in any class or expelled until the completion of elementary education. No child can be subjected to physical punishment or mental harassment. Schools must maintain prescribed pupil-teacher ratios. And no school can charge capitation fees or conduct screening procedures for admission at the elementary level.

The 25 Percent Reservation Clause

One of the most debated provisions of the Act is Section 12(1)(c), which requires private unaided schools to reserve 25 percent of seats in Class 1 for children from economically weaker sections (EWS) and disadvantaged groups. The government reimburses these schools at a rate equivalent to what it spends per child in government schools. This provision was designed to bring disadvantaged children into mainstream private schools and reduce the segregation of the education system along economic lines.

What Changed on the Ground

On the ground, the Act has had real and measurable impacts. Enrolment at the primary level reached near-universal levels in the years following implementation. Physical infrastructure requirements drove investment in toilet construction and school building repair. The prohibition on no-detention — which meant children could not be failed in primary school — reduced dropout rates, though it also sparked a long-running debate about whether it was contributing to poor learning outcomes by removing academic accountability.

Limitations and Gaps

The Act has important limitations. It covers only the 6 to 14 age group, leaving pre-primary and secondary education outside its scope. Many children who enrol in school under the Act's mandate still fail to achieve basic literacy and numeracy — a problem that enrolment data cannot capture. And the implementation of the 25 percent EWS reservation has been uneven, with many private schools finding ways to avoid genuine compliance.
