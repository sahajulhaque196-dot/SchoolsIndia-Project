Population Density and School Access: Why Some Districts Are Left Behind

By Sahajul  ·  Tezpur, Assam  ·  January 2025  ·  10 min read

There is a question buried in the UDISE and census data that does not get asked often enough: are schools actually where the children are? It seems like a basic question, but the answer is more complicated than you might expect. School placement decisions are made by governments, not by market forces, and government decisions are shaped by political factors, historical inertia, and administrative convenience as well as by actual need.

The Geographic Distribution Problem

India's population density varies enormously across districts — from over 10,000 people per square kilometre in some parts of the Indo-Gangetic plain to fewer than 10 in some mountainous and forested districts. This variation creates very different challenges for school access. A densely populated district can have a large number of schools and still have millions of children within easy reach of a school. A sparsely populated district may have far fewer schools, but children may still have to walk long distances because the population is so spread out.

Low Density vs High Density Challenges

In low-density districts — which often include mountainous areas, forest areas, and tribal belts — the challenge is pure geography. Getting to school requires crossing terrain that is difficult and sometimes dangerous, particularly during monsoon season when roads flood and paths become impassable. In these areas, residential schools are often the only practical solution for children above primary age, but residential schools introduce their own challenges around homesickness, safety, and quality oversight.

Urban School Concentration

Counter-intuitively, densely populated urban districts have their own access problems. Schools may be concentrated in better-off neighbourhoods while poorer areas are underserved. Rapid urbanisation has created peri-urban zones — areas at the edges of cities — that have grown faster than educational infrastructure can keep up with. A family that moves to an informal settlement on the outskirts of a city may find that there is no government school within a practical distance.

What Good Planning Looks Like

The districts that have done best at matching school supply to population need tend to have two things in common: active district education planning that uses demographic data to identify gaps, and sufficient administrative capacity to actually implement new school construction or teacher deployment in the right places. Using the school data on Schoolspedia.in alongside population data can give you a rough sense of school density in any district — how many schools exist relative to the population they need to serve.
