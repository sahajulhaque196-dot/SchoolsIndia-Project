The Link Between Sex Ratio and Education: What District Data Reveals About Gender and Schools

By Sahajul  ·  Tezpur, Assam  ·  January 2025  ·  10 min read

When I examine the district-level census data that feeds into Schoolspedia.in, the figures that stop me most often are the sex ratios. The national average of 940 females per 1,000 males in the 2011 Census is a number that carries an enormous weight of meaning — it represents, in aggregate, decades of social attitudes, economic calculations, and individual family decisions about the value of daughters.

Understanding the Two Sex Ratios

The Census records two sex ratios that are both important for understanding gender dynamics in a district. The overall sex ratio measures the number of females per 1,000 males in the total population. The child sex ratio measures the number of girls per 1,000 boys in the 0 to 6 age group. The child sex ratio is in many ways the more disturbing indicator, because it reflects decisions being made right now about daughters — decisions shaped by son preference, economic pressures, and in the worst cases, sex-selective practices.

The Education Connection

The connection between sex ratio and education runs in multiple directions. Districts with higher female literacy rates show better child sex ratios — this is a consistent pattern across the data. Education changes women's economic status, their decision-making power within households, and the social calculus around daughters. But the causation also runs the other way: districts with very skewed sex ratios tend to have fewer women overall, which means fewer mothers with education and fewer adult women in positions that model the value of girls' education.

Districts Where the Ratio Is Improving

Looking at districts where the child sex ratio has improved over the past decade, education is consistently part of the story. The Beti Bachao Beti Padhao initiative, launched in 2015, targeted districts with the most skewed child sex ratios with a combination of awareness programmes and enforcement. Where the programme was implemented seriously and combined with genuine educational outreach, there are signs of improvement. But the improvements are fragile and can reverse when attention moves elsewhere.

The Long Road

The deeper truth that the data points toward is that improving sex ratios requires changing economic conditions alongside changing social attitudes. In communities where daughters are seen primarily as liabilities — requiring dowry, unable to perform ancestral rites, leaving their natal family upon marriage — rational economic arguments for having daughters do not land easily. Education can shift this calculus, but slowly, and only when combined with economic opportunities for women that make the education genuinely worthwhile in material terms.
