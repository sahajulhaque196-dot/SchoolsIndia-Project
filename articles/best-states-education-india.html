Which Indian States Have the Best School Education Systems? An Evidence-Based Look

By Sahajul  ·  Tezpur, Assam  ·  January 2025  ·  10 min read

Every few years, a survey or ranking of state education systems is published, and it inevitably sparks debate. States that rank well point to it with pride; states that rank poorly either dispute the methodology or promise improvement. Having spent years reading the research on this question, I have come to believe that ranking states on education quality is more complicated than it appears — but also that the data does tell us some genuinely important things about which systems are working better than others.

How to Measure Education Quality

Measuring education quality at the state level requires looking at multiple indicators simultaneously. Literacy rates are the most commonly cited, but they are a lagging indicator — today's literacy rate reflects schooling decisions made fifteen or twenty years ago. More current indicators include enrolment rates at different levels, dropout rates (particularly at the critical secondary transition), teacher attendance rates, and learning outcome assessments.

The Strong Performers

Kerala is consistently at the top of most education quality indicators, and it has been for decades. Its literacy rate exceeds 94 percent, male-female literacy gaps are the smallest in the country, and school infrastructure quality is generally high across both government and private aided institutions. The Kerala model — heavy investment in education combined with strong community engagement and a historical tradition of valuing literacy — has been studied extensively and is sometimes held up as a template for other states.

The Middle Ground

Several states occupy interesting middle positions. Tamil Nadu has strong overall literacy and has invested significantly in girls' education, with notable improvements in female secondary enrolment. Himachal Pradesh, despite being a mountainous state with challenging geography, has achieved literacy rates and educational infrastructure quality that punch well above its income level. Gujarat shows high enrolment rates but has faced criticism for learning outcome gaps.

The Challenging Cases

States like Bihar, Rajasthan, and parts of Uttar Pradesh consistently show the lowest educational indicators. These states have large rural populations, high rates of poverty, and historically weak administrative capacity. The combination of high student numbers, under-resourced schools, and significant teacher absenteeism creates a self-reinforcing cycle that is very difficult to break without sustained, serious intervention. Progress has been made in some districts within these states — showing that improvement is possible — but the scale of the challenge remains enormous.
