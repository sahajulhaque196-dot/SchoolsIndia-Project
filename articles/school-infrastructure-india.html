School Infrastructure in India: Toilets, Libraries and the Long Road to Basic Facilities

By Sahajul  ·  Tezpur, Assam  ·  January 2025  ·  10 min read

The first time I walked into a school without a single functional toilet — serving over two hundred children — I was not shocked. I had been writing about rural schools in Assam for several years by then and knew that the gap between what the policy documents promised and what existed on the ground was often enormous. What stayed with me was not the absence of the toilet itself but the matter-of-fact way the headteacher described how girls managed: they simply went home during breaks, losing thirty to forty minutes of learning time each day, and some did not come back.

The Toilet Question

The availability of toilets in schools — particularly separate toilets for girls — has been one of the most discussed infrastructure issues in Indian education policy over the past decade. The Swachh Bharat Abhiyan and its school-focused component, the Swachh Vidyalaya programme, made toilet construction a visible priority. The numbers improved significantly: UDISE data shows dramatic increases in the proportion of schools with functional toilet facilities.

Drinking Water

Drinking water is another basic infrastructure requirement that is present in most schools on paper but is less consistently functional in practice. Hand pumps break down and are not repaired. Pipes corrode. Water purification equipment is installed but not maintained. In areas with groundwater quality issues — fluoride or arsenic contamination — the presence of a water source at school does not guarantee safe drinking water.

Classrooms and Furniture

Classroom infrastructure in Indian government schools ranges from modern, purpose-built buildings to crumbling structures that are technically classified as classrooms but are clearly not safe for occupation. Many rural schools still have students sitting on floors rather than benches and desks. Blackboards are often the primary teaching tool. Lighting in classrooms, particularly in schools without reliable electricity, can make reading and writing genuinely difficult.

Libraries and Technology

Library facilities are present in a minority of government schools, and where they exist, the quality varies enormously. A school listed as having a 'library' in UDISE data might have anything from a well-stocked room with current books to a locked cupboard with outdated textbooks. Computer facilities are increasingly available in secondary schools, though internet connectivity and maintenance remain challenges.

The Infrastructure-Outcome Link

The connection between infrastructure and educational outcomes is real but not simple. Better infrastructure does not automatically produce better learning — that requires good teaching. But bad infrastructure actively undermines learning by making schools uncomfortable, unsafe, or inaccessible. Addressing infrastructure gaps is a necessary but not sufficient condition for improving education quality.
