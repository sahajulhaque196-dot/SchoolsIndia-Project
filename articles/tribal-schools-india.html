Education in Tribal Areas of India: Challenges, Progress and What the Data Shows

By Sahajul  ·  Tezpur, Assam  ·  January 2025  ·  10 min read

Some of the most remote schools I have ever visited were in tribal areas — a single-room structure in a forest clearing in Assam's Karbi Anglong district, where a teacher travelled two hours each way by motorcycle on unpaved roads; a residential school in a hill district of Meghalaya where children boarded during the week because their villages were too far to walk from daily. These visits, spread over fourteen years of writing about education, have given me a deep respect for what teachers and communities in tribal areas manage to accomplish despite extraordinary obstacles.

They have also given me a healthy scepticism about the gap between what the data says and what the ground reality is.

The Scale of Tribal Education in India

India has a significant Scheduled Tribe (ST) population — approximately 10.4 crore people as of the 2011 Census, or about 8.6 percent of the national population. These communities are spread across a wide geographic range, from the tribal belts of central India (Jharkhand, Chhattisgarh, Madhya Pradesh, Odisha) to the hill states of the Northeast, to smaller tribal communities in southern and western India.

Education indicators for Scheduled Tribe populations have historically lagged behind national averages. ST literacy rates in the 2011 Census were around 59 percent nationally, compared to the overall rate of 74 percent. Female ST literacy was considerably lower — around 49 percent. These are aggregates that conceal further variation: Northeastern tribal communities, particularly in Mizoram and Nagaland, have literacy rates well above the national average, while tribal communities in central India often face the sharpest disadvantages.

PESA and the Education Framework

The Panchayats (Extension to Scheduled Areas) Act, commonly known as PESA, is an important piece of legislation that gives tribal communities greater control over governance in Scheduled Areas — including, in principle, some role in local school management. The village data available through Schoolspedia.in includes a flag for whether a village falls within a PESA-governed area, which is a useful starting point for understanding the governance context of schools in those regions.

In practice, the implementation of PESA's education provisions has been inconsistent. In some areas, gram sabhas (village councils) have used their powers effectively to monitor school attendance and advocate for better resources. In others, the formal powers exist on paper but have not translated into meaningful community engagement with schools.

Residential Schools and Ashram Shalas

Recognising that daily commute is a major barrier to school attendance in tribal areas — where villages may be small, dispersed, and connected by poor roads — the government has invested in residential schools for tribal children. Eklavya Model Residential Schools (EMRS) are the flagship central government initiative, providing free residential education to ST students from Class 6 to Class 12.

At the state level, many tribal-majority states run ashram shalas — residential schools typically focused on primary and upper primary education. Assam, Jharkhand, Maharashtra, and Gujarat have extensive ashram shala networks. The quality of these institutions varies enormously. Some are well-staffed, properly funded, and provide a genuinely good education. Others suffer from the same problems that afflict government schools generally — teacher vacancies, poor infrastructure, and inadequate supervision — compounded by the additional vulnerabilities of children living away from home.

Language as a Barrier

One factor that is easy to overlook when looking at tribal education through a data lens is language. Many tribal children enter school speaking a mother tongue that is different from the medium of instruction — which is typically the state's official language or Hindi. This creates an immediate and significant barrier to learning that affects not just comprehension but confidence.

Multilingual education programmes — which begin instruction in the child's mother tongue before gradually transitioning to the official medium — have shown consistently positive results in tribal education contexts. But implementing such programmes requires trained teachers who speak tribal languages, appropriate teaching materials, and sustained commitment from education administrators. All of these are in short supply in most tribal areas.

Looking at the Numbers Honestly

Enrolment rates in tribal areas have improved substantially over the past two decades, partly as a result of the Right to Education Act and partly due to targeted schemes. But enrolment is not the same as attendance, and attendance is not the same as learning. Studies that have measured actual learning outcomes in tribal areas consistently find significant gaps compared to non-tribal populations, even after controlling for socioeconomic factors.

The UDISE data for tribal districts does show a higher proportion of government schools and lower proportion of private schools compared to non-tribal areas — which makes sense given the economics. It also shows lower student-teacher ratios in many tribal blocks, which sounds positive until you realise it is partly a function of low enrolment rather than generous staffing.

Progress has been made. But the ground reality in tribal schools across central India and parts of the Northeast remains one of the most significant challenges in Indian education. Acknowledging that honestly — rather than hiding behind improving enrolment numbers — is the first step toward addressing it properly.
