Girls' Education in India: How Far We Have Come and How Far We Still Have to Go

By Sahajul  ·  Tezpur, Assam  ·  January 2025  ·  10 min read

When I look at the sex ratio data for India's districts — 940 females per 1,000 males at the national level in the 2011 Census, with districts ranging from well above 1,000 to as low as 774 — I often think about what those numbers actually mean in terms of human lives and choices. The child sex ratio, which measures girls per 1,000 boys in the 0 to 6 age group, is in many ways the most revealing figure of all: it captures not just current social attitudes but the decisions families are making right now about the value of daughters.

Education and the child sex ratio are deeply linked. Districts with higher female literacy rates consistently show better child sex ratios. The mechanism is not mysterious: education changes women's economic status, their decision-making power within households, and the social calculus around daughters. Educated women are more likely to reject practices that devalue daughters and more likely to insist on their daughters' schooling in turn.

The Progress Is Real

Let me start with what has genuinely improved, because the progress in girls' education in India over the past three decades is real and substantial, and it deserves to be acknowledged.

Female literacy has risen dramatically. In 1991, the national female literacy rate was around 39 percent. By 2011, it had reached approximately 65 percent. That is a 26 percentage point increase in twenty years — not fast enough, but not nothing either. Enrolment parity between boys and girls at the primary level has been largely achieved nationally, and in many states girls now outnumber boys in secondary school enrolment.

The construction of girls' toilets in government schools — pushed significantly by the Swachh Bharat Abhiyan and earlier initiatives — has had a measurable impact on girls' retention in school, particularly at the secondary level. Studies consistently find that the absence of toilets is a major reason girls drop out around puberty, and that providing them increases attendance.

Where the Challenges Remain

Despite this progress, serious challenges remain, and they are concentrated in specific regions and specific transitions in a girl's educational journey.

The transition from upper primary to secondary school — typically around age 13 to 14 — remains a critical dropout point for girls in many parts of India. At this age, concerns about safety on the way to school, the distance of secondary schools from villages, family pressure around marriage, and the perceived lack of economic return from further education all work against continued schooling. In districts with high rates of child marriage, this dropout often comes right before or immediately after a girl's marriage.

Geography matters enormously here. A girl in an urban area of Kerala or Himachal Pradesh faces a fundamentally different set of constraints than a girl in a remote block of Rajasthan or in a tribal district of Jharkhand. National averages smooth over these variations in ways that can obscure the severity of the problem in specific locations.

The UDISE Data on Girls' Schools

The UDISE database includes information on whether a school is a girls' school, a boys' school, or co-educational. Looking at this data across districts reveals interesting patterns. Girls' schools — single-gender institutions for female students — are more common in states with more conservative social attitudes toward co-education, including parts of Rajasthan, Uttar Pradesh, and Bihar.

The evidence on whether single-gender schools improve outcomes for girls in India is mixed. In communities where families are reluctant to send daughters to co-educational schools, a girls' school can be the difference between a girl attending school and not attending at all. In other contexts, single-gender schooling may reinforce rather than challenge the social segregation that limits girls' opportunities.

The Economic Argument and Why It Matters

One of the most effective arguments for girls' education in communities that are ambivalent about it is the economic one. Educated women earn more, delay marriage, have fewer and healthier children, and invest more in those children's education. These benefits flow not just to individual families but to communities and to the broader economy.

This is not an argument I am entirely comfortable making, because it frames girls' education instrumentally — as valuable because of what it produces — rather than as a right in itself. But in communities where rights-based arguments gain little traction, the economic case has demonstrably moved opinion and behaviour. Using the arguments that work, in the contexts where they work, is pragmatism rather than compromise.

The goal, ultimately, is a country where a girl's access to education is not contingent on her family's attitudes, her district's literacy rate, or the distance to the nearest school. We are not there yet. But looking honestly at where we are — and where the data shows the remaining gaps are deepest — is the necessary starting point.
