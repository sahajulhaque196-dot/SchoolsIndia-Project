Government vs Private Schools in India: What the Data Actually Says

By Sahajul  ·  Tezpur, Assam  ·  January 12, 2025  ·  10 min read

The debate about government versus private schools in India is one of those conversations that never really ends. You will hear strong opinions on both sides — from parents who swear by the private school their child attends, to teachers who argue passionately that the government system has been deliberately starved of resources to make it look worse than it actually is. Having spent fourteen years writing about education across different parts of India, I have come to believe that both sides are often talking past each other — and that the data tells a more complicated, more interesting story than either narrative suggests.

Let me try to lay out what the numbers actually show, and where the numbers fall short of capturing what really matters.

The Scale of the Two Systems

India has approximately 16 lakh schools registered in the UDISE database. Of these, the majority — roughly 70 to 75 percent — are government schools, meaning they are run by state governments, local bodies, or central government departments. The remaining 25 to 30 percent are private schools, either receiving government aid (private aided) or operating entirely independently (private unaided).

This proportion varies dramatically by state. In Kerala, private aided schools have historically played a dominant role and are deeply embedded in the education system. In states like Rajasthan, Madhya Pradesh, and Uttar Pradesh, government schools are the primary — and sometimes the only — option for families in rural areas. In urban centres across the country, private unaided schools have grown rapidly over the past two decades.

Literacy Rates and What They Tell Us

When you look at district-level literacy data from the Census of India, a pattern emerges that is worth examining carefully. Districts with higher rates of private school enrolment do not automatically show higher literacy rates. Kerala, which has near-universal literacy, has achieved this with a system that relies heavily on government and government-aided schools. Bihar, which has one of the lowest literacy rates, has seen rapid growth in private low-cost schools over the past decade.

This does not mean private schools cause low literacy — the causation runs in the opposite direction, with low government school quality driving families toward private alternatives. But it does challenge the simple narrative that private schools are inherently better for educational outcomes at the population level.

What the district data does show clearly is that literacy — particularly female literacy — is most closely correlated with factors like urbanisation, income levels, and social development indices. The type of school management appears to matter less than the overall development context of the district.

Infrastructure and the Rural-Urban Divide

Here is something that often gets lost in the urban-centric media coverage of Indian education: most private schools are urban or semi-urban institutions. For a child in a remote village in Chhattisgarh or a tribal block in Jharkhand, the choice between government and private school often simply does not exist. The government school is the only school. It may be a single-room structure with one teacher handling multiple grades. It may lack a toilet or clean drinking water. But it is there, and without it, there would be no school at all.

This is why I am always cautious about blanket statements like "private schools are better." Better for whom, and compared to what? A government school in a well-funded state like Himachal Pradesh — which has invested seriously in education infrastructure — is not the same as a government school in an underfunded district of Bihar. And a private school charging two thousand rupees a month in a tier-two city is not comparable to a "budget private school" in a semi-rural area that charges a few hundred rupees and operates out of a rented house.

Teacher Attendance and Accountability

One of the most persistent criticisms of government schools is teacher absenteeism. There is genuine evidence for this problem, and it is serious. Studies conducted across multiple states have found that teacher absence rates in government schools are significantly higher than in private schools, particularly in rural and remote areas.

The reasons are structural. Government school teachers are civil servants with job security and a salary that arrives regardless of whether they show up or not. This is not an excuse — it is a governance problem that requires better monitoring and accountability mechanisms. And to be fair, many government school teachers work in extraordinarily difficult conditions: remote postings, poor infrastructure, no support staff, and students from deeply disadvantaged backgrounds who need more support, not less.

Private schools, particularly unaided ones, have stronger accountability mechanisms precisely because they depend on fees. A teacher who consistently does not show up risks the school losing students and therefore revenue. But this accountability cuts both ways — it also means that private schools in competitive markets may prioritise exam results over genuine learning, leading to the rote-memorisation culture that critics rightly identify as a problem.

The Right to Education Act and Its Impact

The Right to Education Act of 2009 was a landmark piece of legislation that made free and compulsory education a fundamental right for children between six and fourteen years of age. One of its key provisions — Section 12(1)(c) — required private unaided schools to reserve 25 percent of their seats for children from economically weaker sections and disadvantaged groups, with reimbursement from the government.

The implementation of this provision has been patchy across states. Some states have implemented it reasonably well; others have allowed private schools to find workarounds. But where it has worked, it has created something genuinely interesting: classrooms where children from very different economic backgrounds learn side by side. The evidence on whether this improves outcomes for disadvantaged children is mixed, but the social impact of integration should not be dismissed.

What Should Parents Actually Do?

If you are a parent trying to decide between a government and a private school for your child, here is my honest advice: visit both. Look at the infrastructure, talk to teachers if you can, speak to parents of children who already attend, and look at the school's UDISE data to understand its basic profile. The school type — government or private — matters less than the specific quality of the school in front of you.

In many parts of India, a well-run government school is genuinely the better option. In others, a decent private school is worth the fees. There is no universal answer, and anyone who tells you there is has probably not spent much time in Indian schools outside their own city.

Use the school pages on this website as a starting point. Find the schools in your block or district, compare their categories and management types, and then go and see them for yourself. Data can tell you what a school is. Only a visit can tell you what it is actually like.
