The Mid-Day Meal Scheme: How It Works and Why It Matters for Rural Children

By Sahajul  ·  Tezpur, Assam  ·  January 2025  ·  10 min read

Every time I visit a government primary school in rural Assam, one of the first things I notice is the smell of cooking coming from behind the school building. It is usually rice and dal, sometimes with vegetables, occasionally with an egg. The mid-day meal — hot, freshly cooked, and served to every child who attends school that day — is so deeply embedded in the daily rhythm of rural primary schools that it is easy to forget what a significant policy achievement it represents.

What the Scheme Is

The PM POSHAN scheme (formerly known as the Mid-Day Meal Scheme) is one of the largest school meal programmes in the world. It provides free cooked meals to children enrolled in government and government-aided primary and upper primary schools across India. The programme is centrally sponsored, meaning the central government provides funds for food grains and some cooking costs, while state governments contribute the rest and manage actual implementation.

The Nutrition Dimension

For children in poor rural households, the mid-day meal may be the most nutritious meal they receive in a day. Many rural families — particularly in districts with high rates of agricultural labour and seasonal income variation — go through periods of genuine food insecurity. A hot, balanced meal at school ensures that children receive at least some nutritional support on school days. Studies have documented measurable improvements in children's nutritional status in areas with strong mid-day meal programmes.

The Attendance Impact

The attendance impact of the mid-day meal scheme is well-documented and substantial. Before the scheme was implemented nationally, research found that the promise of a meal increased daily attendance, particularly among girls and among children from the poorest households. For a family deciding whether to send a child to school or have them work at home or in the fields, the tangible benefit of a meal tips the calculation toward school attendance. This is not a flattering reason for children to attend school, but it is a real and important one.

Implementation Challenges

Implementation quality varies enormously across states and districts. Food safety issues — contaminated meals, inadequate cooking facilities, problems with food grain storage — have caused illness and, in a small number of tragic cases, deaths. These incidents receive significant media attention and have led to tightened quality control requirements. But the day-to-day reality in most schools is more mundane: underpaid cooks, insufficient fuel or cooking equipment, delays in food grain delivery, and the constant logistical challenge of feeding hundreds of children daily in schools that often lack proper kitchen infrastructure.
