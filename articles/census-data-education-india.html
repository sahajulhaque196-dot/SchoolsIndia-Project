What India's Census Data Tells Us About the Country's Education Gap

By Sahajul  ·  Tezpur, Assam  ·  January 2025  ·  10 min read

The Census of India is one of the most comprehensive demographic surveys conducted anywhere in the world. Every ten years, enumerators visit every household in the country and record a vast range of information: population, age, gender, religion, caste, literacy, occupation, and much more. When you combine this census data with the UDISE school database — as we have done on Schoolspedia.in — you get a picture of Indian education that is richer and more revealing than either dataset alone can provide.

The Literacy Question in the Census

The Census asks a simple question to determine literacy: can you read and write a simple message in any language? This is a lower bar than functional literacy or educational attainment, but it is still a meaningful baseline. The Census literacy data gives us the most geographically granular picture available of basic literacy across India, with figures available at the district level and, in some cases, at the tehsil or town level.

What Religion and Caste Data Shows

The Census data includes information on the religious and caste composition of each district's population. When you look at this alongside literacy data, patterns emerge that are both expected and sometimes surprising. Districts with high proportions of Scheduled Caste and Scheduled Tribe populations tend to show lower literacy rates, reflecting centuries of systematic exclusion from education. But there are also districts where SC and ST literacy rates have improved rapidly — showing that historical disadvantage is not destiny.

The Workers Data and Its Implications

The Census also records the occupational status of the population — the proportion who are cultivators, agricultural labourers, other workers, and non-workers. Districts with high proportions of agricultural labourers — people who work on others' land for wages — tend to show lower literacy rates and educational attainment. This reflects the economic reality that agricultural labour households have less resources for education and more immediate economic pressure to keep children at work rather than in school.

Reading the Numbers Together

When you look at school density data from UDISE alongside census population data, you can calculate rough figures for how many schools serve each thousand people of school-going age in each district. This exercise reveals which districts are genuinely underserved relative to their school-age population — not just in absolute terms, but relative to the size of their population. Districts with low school density and low literacy are the places where targeted education investment would have the highest impact.
